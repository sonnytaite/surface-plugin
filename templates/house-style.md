# House style — briefs and digests

> This file is yours. /share writes to whatever this says; edit it to change the voice.
> The default below is a compact executive-briefing style in the spirit of the best
> magazine feature writing: strong format, fast entry points, no padding.

## The shapes

**Brief** (one idea, ~800–1400 words):

1. **Summary** — a 3–4 sentence abstract a reader can stop after.
2. **Idea in Brief** — three labelled beats: *Problem / Solution / Result* (or
   *Problem / Idea / What it means for you* when nothing is built yet). A box, not prose.
3. **The arc** — context → the insight → how it plays out → limits. Subheads every few
   paragraphs. One idea per piece; resist the second idea, link to it instead.
4. **Where This Stands** — one plain paragraph stating the stage (see ladder below) and
   what would move it a rung.
5. **What you can use tomorrow** — 2–4 concrete take-aways for a peer.
6. **The Bottom Line** — two sentences, no new information.
7. **Lexicon** — coined terms used, each with a one-line definition.

**Digest entry** (per idea, ~120–180 words): synopsis paragraph (problem → insight →
what it means for you) + a stage line + a pointer to the full brief if one exists.
The digest as a whole reads like a magazine's back-page executive summaries: the busy
reader covers the entire issue in two pages.

## The stage ladder (use these words exactly)

`thought piece → vision → options explored → prototype → in production`

Every brief and digest entry carries its stage. Write each thing AS the stage it is —
a vision written as a vision is credible; a vision written as a product is a lie with
a delay on it. Never announce honesty ("to be honest…"); just be at the right rung.

## Voice

- Plain words. Short sentences carrying one load each. Explain every coined term on
  first use — the reader was not in the room.
- Concrete over abstract: a number, an example, a named thing beats an adjective.
- Easy on em-dashes and colons; no hype adjectives (revolutionary, game-changing);
  no symmetric bullet-triads for their own sake.
- First person is fine and human ("I tried X and it failed") — the author is a person
  sharing work, not an institution issuing a statement.
- The reader's question is always "what does this mean for me?" — answer it explicitly.
